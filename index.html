📚 Day 1: Hook & Thesis (The Foundation)

To build a strong essay, you need a strong introduction. An introduction has a three-step formula:

1. The Hook

Grabs the reader's attention using a rhetorical question, a bold statement, or vivid imagery.

2. Background Context

Explains the context or debate surrounding your topic in 1-2 sentences.

3. Thesis Statement

State your clear position and list the reasons you'll explore (often using a tricolon list of three).

📊 Day 2: Building the Argument (PEEL Structure)

Each paragraph in the body of your essay needs to focus on one clear argument. We use the evidence-based PEEL framework to structure them:

P - Point

A clear topic sentence stating the main reason supporting your thesis.

E - Evidence

Include examples, daily scenarios, facts, or observations to make it real.

E - Explanation

Explore why the point matters and the impact it has on people's lives.

L - Link

Tie your paragraph back to your thesis position to prove your point.

🎨 Day 3: Drafting the Body (Rhetorical Toolkit)

Now, let's turn your outlines from Day 2 into fully-formed body paragraphs. Persuasive writing relies on a **Rhetorical Toolkit** to engage readers and make arguments memorable.

💡 Persuasive Devices Checklist:

🔘 Emotive Language: Upgrade neutral words (e.g. "neglected and starving" instead of "thin").
🔘 Rhetorical Questions: Challenge the reader (e.g. "How would you feel?").
🔘 Tricolon: Power of Three (e.g. "Unjust, unequal, and unacceptable").
🔘 Similes/Metaphors: Direct comparisons (e.g. "Culture is a deep-rooted tree").

🔍 Upgrade Example: Neutral vs. Enhanced

Neutral draft

"When people are mean to others, it makes the school bad. People should help when books are dropped."

Enhanced (Rhetorical Toolkit)

"Vicious behavior poisons our school community. Imagine walking down the hallway and seeing books drop while a crowd laughs. Will you look away, or be the hero who steps in?"

🏁 Day 4: The Conclusion & Google Classroom

A conclusion binds your whole essay together. It has a **three-step formula**:

1. Restate Position

State your thesis position again, but use fresh words.

2. Summarise Main Arguments

Remind the reader of the key points you made in your body paragraphs.

3. Call to Action (Strong Ending)

Issue a challenge or leave a final thought. Use repetition or a question.
